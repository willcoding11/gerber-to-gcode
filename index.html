Machine

Printed on the box or in the Luban software. Used to warn if your board won't fit.

Cutting bit

V-bits are what you want for PCB traces — a narrow point that isolates thin lines. Flat mills are for drilling and cutting the outline.
Printed on the bit packaging. Smaller angle = finer traces but weaker tip. 30° is a common default.
For V-bits this is the flat at the tip (~0.1 mm). For end mills, the cutter diameter (e.g. 0.8 mm for drilling, 2 mm for cutouts). On the bit sleeve or KiCad BOM for the drill file.
Use a flat end mill with diameter matching your smallest drill hole. If holes differ in size, use the smallest; bigger holes get milled as circles.
Used to cut the board outline out of the stock. 2 mm flat end mill is typical.

Depths & passes

Just deeper than copper. Standard 1 oz copper is 0.035 mm; 0.05–0.1 mm is safe.
Gap to mill around each trace. 0.2–0.4 mm. Wider = safer electrical isolation, but eats small pad spacing.
Standard PCBs are 1.6 mm. Measure with calipers if unsure.
How far past the bottom of the board the cutout bit goes, so the board releases cleanly.
Max depth per pass when cutting the outline. Smaller = gentler on bit. 0.3–0.5 mm for FR4.
Travel height for rapid moves. 2–5 mm. Must clear any clamps.
Tabs hold the board to the stock during the last cut. 3–6 for small boards. Cut them away afterwards with a knife.

Speeds

Horizontal cutting speed. 200–400 for PCB traces.
Downward cutting speed. 60–100. Too fast snaps small bits.
Rapid moves at Safe Z. Snapmaker handles ~3000.
Snapmaker CNC max ~12000. Higher = cleaner cut.

Origin

Whichever matches how you set X0/Y0 on the Snapmaker. Bottom-left is easiest — jog the bit to the corner of your stock.
Only flip if you're milling the back side of the PCB.
